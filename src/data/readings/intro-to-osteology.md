# Introduction to Osteology

Osteology is the branch of anatomy concerned with the study of bones. The adult human skeleton consists of **206 bones**, divided into two main divisions: the **axial skeleton** and the **appendicular skeleton**. Bone serves as the rigid framework of the body, but it is far from inert — it is a dynamic, living tissue that undergoes continuous remodelling throughout life.

## Functions of the Skeleton

The skeletal system serves six principal functions:

1. **Support** — provides the structural framework that supports soft tissues and maintains body shape
2. **Protection** — shields vital organs (cranium protects the brain, thoracic cage protects heart and lungs, vertebral column protects the spinal cord)
3. **Movement** — bones act as levers for skeletal muscles; joints serve as fulcrums
4. **Mineral storage** — the skeleton is the body's primary reservoir for calcium (~99% of body calcium) and phosphorus (~85%), releasing them into the blood as needed
5. **Haematopoiesis** — red bone marrow within cancellous bone produces red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets
6. **Energy storage** — yellow bone marrow stores triglycerides as an energy reserve

## Axial Skeleton

The axial skeleton (80 bones) forms the central longitudinal axis of the body.

### Skull

The skull consists of **22 bones** — 8 cranial bones forming the cranial vault (calvaria) and cranial base, and 14 facial bones.

**Cranial bones:**

| Bone | Number | Key Features |
|------|--------|-------------|
| Frontal | 1 | Forms the forehead, superior orbital margins, and anterior cranial fossa; contains the frontal sinuses |
| Parietal | 2 | Form the superior and lateral walls of the cranium; joined at the sagittal suture |
| Temporal | 2 | House the middle and inner ear; contain the mastoid process, styloid process, zygomatic process, and mandibular fossa |
| Occipital | 1 | Forms the posterior cranium and posterior cranial fossa; contains the foramen magnum and occipital condyles |
| Sphenoid | 1 | Bat-shaped bone spanning the middle cranial fossa; contains the sella turcica (housing the pituitary gland), greater and lesser wings, pterygoid processes |
| Ethmoid | 1 | Forms part of the anterior cranial fossa, nasal septum (perpendicular plate), and lateral nasal walls (superior and middle conchae); contains the cribriform plate |

**Facial bones:**

| Bone | Number | Key Features |
|------|--------|-------------|
| Maxilla | 2 | Form the upper jaw, anterior hard palate, floor of orbit; contain the maxillary sinuses (largest paranasal sinuses) |
| Palatine | 2 | Form the posterior hard palate and part of the nasal cavity and orbit |
| Zygomatic | 2 | Form the cheek prominences and part of the lateral orbital wall |
| Lacrimal | 2 | Smallest facial bones; form part of the medial orbital wall; contain the lacrimal fossa housing the lacrimal sac |
| Nasal | 2 | Form the bridge of the nose |
| Inferior nasal concha | 2 | Project from the lateral nasal walls; increase turbulence of inhaled air |
| Vomer | 1 | Forms the inferior part of the nasal septum |
| Mandible | 1 | The only moveable skull bone; largest and strongest facial bone; contains the mental foramen, mandibular foramen, condylar and coronoid processes |

**Key sutures:**
- **Coronal suture** — between frontal and parietal bones
- **Sagittal suture** — between the two parietal bones
- **Lambdoid suture** — between parietal bones and occipital bone
- **Squamous suture** — between parietal and temporal bones

**Fontanelles** (in neonates): the anterior fontanelle (bregma, closes ~18 months) and posterior fontanelle (lambda, closes ~2 months) are membranous gaps at suture intersections that allow moulding of the skull during birth and accommodate brain growth.

### Vertebral Column

The vertebral column consists of **26 bones** (33 vertebrae before fusion) arranged in four curvatures:

- **Cervical** (7 vertebrae, C1–C7) — lordotic curve; supports the head. C1 (atlas) has no body and articulates with the occipital condyles to allow nodding. C2 (axis) has the dens (odontoid process) that acts as a pivot for head rotation. C7 (vertebra prominens) has a long, palpable spinous process.
- **Thoracic** (12 vertebrae, T1–T12) — kyphotic curve; articulate with ribs via costal facets on the body and transverse processes. Spinous processes angle sharply downward.
- **Lumbar** (5 vertebrae, L1–L5) — lordotic curve; largest vertebral bodies bearing the most weight. Short, broad spinous processes project directly posteriorly (important for lumbar puncture access).
- **Sacrum** (5 fused vertebrae) — kyphotic curve; wedge-shaped; articulates with the hip bones at the sacroiliac joints. Contains the sacral canal and four pairs of sacral foramina for spinal nerve exit.
- **Coccyx** (3–5 fused vertebrae) — the vestigial tailbone; attachment site for pelvic floor muscles and ligaments.

**Typical vertebra structure:** body (centrum), vertebral arch (pedicles + laminae), vertebral foramen, spinous process, two transverse processes, four articular processes (superior and inferior facets). The intervertebral foramina between adjacent pedicles transmit spinal nerves.

**Intervertebral discs** consist of an outer **annulus fibrosus** (concentric layers of fibrocartilage) and an inner **nucleus pulposus** (gelatinous, remnant of the notochord). They account for ~25% of the column's length and act as shock absorbers. Herniation occurs when the nucleus pulposus protrudes through the annulus, typically posterolaterally, compressing adjacent spinal nerve roots.

### Thoracic Cage

The thoracic cage (25 bones) protects thoracic and upper abdominal organs and provides attachment for respiratory muscles.

- **Sternum** (1) — composed of the manubrium, body, and xiphoid process. The sternal angle (angle of Louis, junction of manubrium and body) is a key landmark at the level of the T4/T5 intervertebral disc, the second costal cartilage, and the bifurcation of the trachea.
- **Ribs** (12 pairs):
  - **True ribs** (1–7) — attach directly to the sternum via their own costal cartilage
  - **False ribs** (8–10) — attach indirectly to the sternum via the costal cartilage of rib 7
  - **Floating ribs** (11–12) — have no anterior attachment to the sternum

Each typical rib has a head (articulates with vertebral bodies), neck, tubercle (articulates with the transverse process), and a shaft with the costal groove on its inferior inner surface protecting the intercostal neurovascular bundle (vein, artery, nerve — VAN, from superior to inferior).

### Hyoid Bone

The hyoid bone (1) is unique — it does not articulate with any other bone. It is suspended by muscles and ligaments from the styloid processes of the temporal bones. It supports the tongue and serves as an attachment for muscles of swallowing and speech. Fracture of the hyoid may indicate strangulation in forensic contexts.

### Auditory Ossicles

Three tiny paired bones in the middle ear cavity form a chain that transmits sound vibrations from the tympanic membrane to the inner ear:

- **Malleus** (hammer) — attached to the tympanic membrane
- **Incus** (anvil) — intermediate bone
- **Stapes** (stirrup) — footplate sits in the oval window; smallest bone in the body

## Appendicular Skeleton

The appendicular skeleton (126 bones) includes the limbs and the girdles that attach them to the axial skeleton.

### Pectoral (Shoulder) Girdle

The pectoral girdle (4 bones total) is designed for **mobility** over stability, connecting each upper limb to the axial skeleton via only one bony articulation — the sternoclavicular joint.

- **Clavicle** (2) — S-shaped; acts as a strut holding the shoulder laterally. Most commonly fractured bone in the body (usually at the junction of the middle and lateral thirds). Subclavian vessels and brachial plexus pass posterior to the middle third.
- **Scapula** (2) — triangular flat bone on the posterior thorax. Key features include the spine, acromion, coracoid process, glenoid cavity (shallow socket for the humeral head), supraspinous fossa, infraspinous fossa, and subscapular fossa. The scapula is held in place entirely by muscles (serratus anterior, trapezius, rhomboids, levator scapulae).

### Upper Limb

**Humerus** — longest and largest bone of the upper limb. Key features:
- Proximal: head (articulates with glenoid cavity), anatomical neck, greater and lesser tubercles, intertubercular sulcus (bicipital groove), surgical neck (common fracture site; axillary nerve at risk)
- Shaft: deltoid tuberosity, radial (spiral) groove (radial nerve runs here — vulnerable in mid-shaft fractures)
- Distal: medial and lateral epicondyles (medial epicondyle has the ulnar nerve posterior to it — the "funny bone"), capitulum (articulates with radial head), trochlea (articulates with ulna), olecranon fossa, coronoid fossa

**Radius** — lateral forearm bone in anatomical position. Head articulates with capitulum and radial notch of the ulna. The radial tuberosity is the insertion of the biceps brachii. The distal end is broad, forming the main articulation with the carpal bones at the wrist. Colles' fracture (distal radius fracture with dorsal displacement) is common from falls on an outstretched hand (FOOSH).

**Ulna** — medial forearm bone. The olecranon forms the point of the elbow. The coronoid process and trochlear notch articulate with the trochlea of the humerus. The styloid process is at the distal end.

**Carpals** (8 per wrist, arranged in two rows):
- Proximal row (lateral to medial): scaphoid, lunate, triquetrum, pisiform
- Distal row (lateral to medial): trapezium, trapezoid, capitate, hamate

The **scaphoid** is the most commonly fractured carpal bone (FOOSH injuries). It has a retrograde blood supply — fractures through the waist can lead to avascular necrosis of the proximal pole. Scaphoid fractures may not appear on initial X-rays and may need repeat imaging or MRI.

**Metacarpals** (5) and **phalanges** (14) — each finger has proximal, middle, and distal phalanges; the thumb has only proximal and distal.

### Pelvic Girdle

The pelvic girdle (2 hip bones) is designed for **stability** and weight transfer. Each hip bone (os coxae) is formed by the fusion of three bones:

- **Ilium** — largest portion; the iliac crest is a palpable landmark. The anterior superior iliac spine (ASIS) is a key clinical landmark (inguinal ligament attaches here; used for McBurney's point measurement).
- **Ischium** — posteroinferior portion; the ischial tuberosity ("sit bones") bears body weight when sitting and is the origin of the hamstrings. The ischial spine separates the greater and lesser sciatic foramina.
- **Pubis** — anterior portion; the two pubic bones meet at the pubic symphysis (secondary cartilaginous joint).

The **acetabulum** is the deep socket formed where all three bones meet, articulating with the femoral head. The **obturator foramen** is the large opening bordered by the pubis and ischium, largely covered by the obturator membrane.

**Male vs. female pelvis:**

| Feature | Male | Female |
|---------|------|--------|
| General structure | Narrow, deep, heavy | Wide, shallow, light |
| Pelvic inlet | Heart-shaped | Oval/round |
| Subpubic angle | <70° | >80° |
| Ischial spines | Project more medially | Less projecting |
| Sacrum | Longer, narrower, more curved | Shorter, wider, less curved |
| Pelvic outlet | Narrower | Wider (adapted for childbirth) |

### Lower Limb

**Femur** — longest, heaviest, and strongest bone in the body. Key features:
- Proximal: head (with fovea for the ligament of the head), neck (common fracture site in the elderly/osteoporosis; intracapsular fractures can disrupt blood supply from the medial circumflex femoral artery causing avascular necrosis), greater trochanter (palpable laterally; attachment of gluteal muscles), lesser trochanter (attachment of iliopsoas), intertrochanteric line (anterior) and intertrochanteric crest (posterior)
- Shaft: linea aspera (ridge on posterior surface for muscle attachments — adductors, vastus medialis, short head of biceps femoris)
- Distal: medial and lateral condyles (articulate with tibia), medial and lateral epicondyles, intercondylar fossa, patellar surface

**Patella** — largest sesamoid bone; develops within the tendon of the quadriceps femoris. Improves mechanical advantage of the quadriceps by increasing the lever arm at the knee.

**Tibia** — the weight-bearing bone of the leg. The medial and lateral condyles articulate with the femoral condyles. The tibial tuberosity is the insertion of the patellar ligament (quadriceps mechanism). The medial surface is subcutaneous along its entire length — making it vulnerable to direct trauma (shin). The medial malleolus forms the medial prominence of the ankle.

**Fibula** — a non-weight-bearing bone that serves primarily for muscle attachment and forms the lateral wall of the ankle joint. The lateral malleolus extends more distally than the medial malleolus, which is why ankle sprains most commonly involve lateral ligament (inversion) injuries.

**Tarsals** (7 per foot):
- **Talus** — articulates with tibia and fibula superiorly (ankle joint) and calcaneus inferiorly. No muscles attach to the talus. Largely covered in articular cartilage. Vulnerable to avascular necrosis after fracture due to retrograde blood supply.
- **Calcaneus** — the heel bone; largest tarsal. Receives body weight from the talus and transmits it to the ground. The calcaneal (Achilles) tendon inserts on the posterior surface.
- **Navicular, cuboid, and three cuneiforms** (medial, intermediate, lateral) — form the midfoot.

**Arches of the foot:**
- **Medial longitudinal arch** (calcaneus → talus → navicular → cuneiforms → metatarsals 1–3) — higher, more mobile; keystone is the talus; spring ligament supports it inferiorly
- **Lateral longitudinal arch** (calcaneus → cuboid → metatarsals 4–5) — lower, more rigid; rests on the ground during standing
- **Transverse arch** (across the cuneiforms and cuboid) — maintained by the tendon of fibularis longus and the interosseous ligaments

The arches distribute weight, absorb shock, and create a lever for propulsion during gait. Collapse of the medial arch results in **pes planus** (flat foot).

## Bone Classification

Bones are classified by shape into five categories:

| Type | Examples | Characteristics |
|------|----------|----------------|
| Long | Femur, humerus, radius, ulna, tibia, fibula, metacarpals, metatarsals, phalanges | Greater length than width; shaft (diaphysis) with two expanded ends (epiphyses); medullary cavity containing marrow |
| Short | Carpals, tarsals | Roughly cube-shaped; mostly cancellous (spongy) bone with a thin cortex of compact bone |
| Flat | Sternum, ribs, skull bones, scapula | Thin, flattened; two layers of compact bone (tables) sandwiching cancellous bone (diploë in skull) |
| Irregular | Vertebrae, hip bones, facial bones | Complex shapes that don't fit other categories |
| Sesamoid | Patella, pisiform | Develop within tendons; protect tendons from excessive wear and alter the direction of pull |

## Bone Structure

### Macroscopic Structure of a Long Bone

- **Diaphysis** — the tubular shaft; walls of thick compact (cortical) bone surrounding the **medullary cavity** (contains yellow marrow in adults)
- **Epiphyses** — the expanded proximal and distal ends; mostly cancellous (spongy/trabecular) bone covered by a thin shell of compact bone; articular surfaces covered by hyaline cartilage
- **Metaphysis** — transitional zone between diaphysis and epiphysis; location of the **epiphyseal plate** (growth plate) in growing bone, replaced by the **epiphyseal line** after growth ceases
- **Periosteum** — dense connective tissue membrane covering the external surface (except at articular cartilage). Outer fibrous layer is protective; inner osteogenic (cambium) layer contains osteoprogenitor cells critical for appositional growth, fracture repair, and bone remodelling. Richly innervated — periosteal stripping is extremely painful.
- **Endosteum** — thin membrane lining the medullary cavity and trabeculae; also contains osteoprogenitor cells

### Microscopic Structure

**Compact bone** is organized into **osteons** (Haversian systems):
- Central (Haversian) canal — runs longitudinally, contains blood vessels and nerves
- Concentric lamellae — rings of calcified matrix surrounding the central canal
- Lacunae — small spaces between lamellae housing osteocytes
- Canaliculi — tiny channels radiating from lacunae, connecting osteocytes to each other and to the central canal; allow nutrient exchange
- Perforating (Volkmann's) canals — connect adjacent Haversian canals and the periosteum to the endosteum

**Cancellous (spongy) bone** consists of trabeculae — thin plates and struts arranged along lines of mechanical stress (following Wolff's Law). The spaces between trabeculae are filled with red or yellow bone marrow. No osteons — osteocytes receive nutrients by diffusion from marrow blood vessels through canaliculi.

### Bone Cells

| Cell Type | Function | Origin |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| Osteoprogenitor cells | Mitotically active stem cells; differentiate into osteoblasts | Mesenchymal stem cells |
| Osteoblasts | Synthesise and secrete osteoid (unmineralised bone matrix — Type I collagen + proteoglycans); regulate mineralisation | Osteoprogenitor cells |
| Osteocytes | Mature bone cells trapped in lacunae; maintain bone matrix; act as mechanosensors detecting strain; communicate via gap junctions in canaliculi | Osteoblasts that become embedded |
| Osteoclasts | Large, multinucleated cells that resorb bone by secreting H⁺ ions (dissolve mineral) and lysosomal enzymes (digest organic matrix) into a sealed resorption pit (Howship's lacuna) | Monocyte/macrophage lineage (haematopoietic) |

### Bone Matrix Composition

- **Organic component (~35%)** — primarily Type I collagen fibres, plus proteoglycans and glycoproteins (e.g., osteocalcin, osteonectin). Provides **tensile strength** and flexibility.
- **Inorganic component (~65%)** — primarily hydroxyapatite crystals [Ca₁₀(PO₄)₆(OH)₂] deposited along collagen fibres. Provides **compressive strength** and rigidity.

This combination makes bone both strong and resilient — comparable to reinforced concrete (collagen = steel rebar, hydroxyapatite = concrete).

## Ossification

Bone development occurs via two mechanisms:

### Intramembranous Ossification

- Forms **flat bones** of the skull, mandible, and clavicle
- Mesenchymal cells differentiate directly into osteoblasts within a fibrous membrane
- Osteoblasts secrete osteoid that mineralises to form trabeculae of woven bone
- Woven bone is later remodelled into lamellar bone
- Fontanelles are the remnants of unossified membrane at birth

### Endochondral Ossification

- Forms most bones of the skeleton (long bones, vertebrae, pelvis, etc.)
- A hyaline cartilage model forms first, then is progressively replaced by bone
- Sequence: the **primary ossification centre** appears in the diaphysis (mid-gestation); **secondary ossification centres** appear in the epiphyses (around birth and during childhood); the **epiphyseal plate** maintains longitudinal growth until closure (typically 18–25 years)
- Growth in length occurs at the epiphyseal plate through zones: reserve zone → proliferative zone (chondrocyte division) → hypertrophic zone (chondrocyte enlargement) → calcification zone (cartilage matrix calcifies, chondrocytes die) → ossification zone (osteoblasts lay down bone on calcified cartilage scaffolding)

## Bone Markings

Understanding surface features of bones is essential for identifying muscle attachment sites, joint surfaces, and passageways for nerves and vessels.

### Projections for Muscle and Ligament Attachment

- **Tuberosity** — large, rounded, roughened area (e.g., deltoid tuberosity of humerus, tibial tuberosity)
- **Tubercle** — small, rounded projection (e.g., greater and lesser tubercles of humerus, pubic tubercle)
- **Crest** — narrow ridge of bone (e.g., iliac crest, linea aspera)
- **Trochanter** — very large, blunt projection found only on the femur (greater and lesser trochanters)
- **Epicondyle** — raised area above a condyle (e.g., medial and lateral epicondyles of humerus and femur)
- **Spine** — sharp, slender projection (e.g., spine of scapula, ischial spine, ASIS)
- **Line** — less prominent ridge than a crest (e.g., linea aspera, intertrochanteric line)
- **Process** — any bony prominence (general term)

### Articular Surfaces

- **Head** — rounded articular projection supported on a neck (e.g., femoral head, humeral head, radial head)
- **Condyle** — rounded articular surface, often paired (e.g., femoral condyles, occipital condyles)
- **Facet** — small, flat articular surface (e.g., articular facets of vertebrae, costal facets)
- **Trochlea** — spool-shaped articular surface (e.g., trochlea of humerus)

### Depressions and Openings

- **Foramen** — round or oval opening through bone for vessels/nerves (e.g., foramen magnum, obturator foramen, mental foramen)
- **Fossa** — shallow depression (e.g., mandibular fossa, olecranon fossa, subscapular fossa)
- **Meatus (canal)** — canal-like passageway (e.g., external acoustic meatus, carotid canal)
- **Fissure** — narrow, slit-like opening (e.g., superior orbital fissure, inferior orbital fissure)
- **Notch** — indentation at the edge of a bone (e.g., greater sciatic notch, jugular notch of manubrium)
- **Sulcus (groove)** — furrow along a bone surface for a tendon, nerve, or vessel (e.g., radial groove of humerus, intertubercular sulcus)
- **Sinus** — air-filled cavity within a bone (e.g., paranasal sinuses — frontal, maxillary, ethmoidal, sphenoidal)

## Blood Supply to Bone

Long bones receive blood from multiple sources:

1. **Nutrient artery** — the principal supply; enters through the nutrient foramen in the diaphysis, divides into ascending and descending branches in the medullary cavity
2. **Periosteal arteries** — supply the outer third of compact bone via Volkmann's canals
3. **Metaphyseal arteries** — supply the metaphysis
4. **Epiphyseal arteries** — supply the epiphysis (important because in adults, these are the main supply to the femoral head via the medial circumflex femoral artery)

Disruption of blood supply (e.g., femoral neck fracture interrupting the medial circumflex femoral artery) leads to **avascular necrosis** — death of bone tissue due to ischaemia.

## Bone Remodelling

Bone remodelling is a continuous, lifelong process balancing bone resorption (osteoclasts) and bone formation (osteoblasts). Approximately 10% of skeletal mass is replaced annually.

**The remodelling cycle:**
1. **Activation** — osteoclast precursors are recruited to the remodelling site
2. **Resorption** — osteoclasts excavate a resorption pit over ~2–4 weeks
3. **Reversal** — transition from resorption to formation; mononuclear cells prepare the surface
4. **Formation** — osteoblasts deposit new osteoid that mineralises over ~4–6 months
5. **Quiescence** — surface returns to resting state with lining cells

**Wolff's Law** states that bone adapts its structure to the mechanical loads placed upon it — increased loading leads to increased bone density along lines of stress, while decreased loading (immobilisation, microgravity) leads to bone loss.

**Hormonal regulation:**

| Hormone | Effect on Bone | Mechanism |
|---------|---------------|-----------|
| Parathyroid hormone (PTH) | Increases blood Ca²⁺ | Stimulates osteoclast activity (indirectly via osteoblasts); increases renal Ca²⁺ reabsorption |
| Calcitonin | Decreases blood Ca²⁺ | Inhibits osteoclast activity; minor physiological role |
| Calcitriol (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D₃) | Increases blood Ca²⁺ | Increases intestinal Ca²⁺ absorption; required for proper mineralisation |
| Growth hormone / IGF-1 | Stimulates growth | Stimulates epiphyseal plate chondrocyte proliferation and osteoblast activity |
| Oestrogen | Protective | Inhibits osteoclast activity; loss at menopause accelerates bone resorption → osteoporosis |
| Testosterone | Anabolic | Stimulates osteoblast activity and periosteal bone growth |
| Thyroid hormones (T₃/T₄) | Permissive for growth | Necessary for normal skeletal development; excess → accelerated resorption |
| Cortisol (excess) | Catabolic | Inhibits osteoblast activity; prolonged glucocorticoid use → osteoporosis |

## Fracture Repair

Fracture healing proceeds through four overlapping phases:

1. **Haematoma formation** (hours) — blood from ruptured vessels clots at the fracture site, forming a fracture haematoma. Inflammatory cells infiltrate, removing debris. The haematoma provides a scaffold and signalling molecules to initiate repair.

2. **Soft (fibrocartilaginous) callus** (days to weeks) — fibroblasts produce collagen fibres spanning the gap. Chondroblasts produce fibrocartilage in areas of low oxygen tension. Periosteal and endosteal osteoblasts begin producing woven bone near the fracture edges (external and internal callus). The soft callus provides initial stabilisation.

3. **Hard (bony) callus** (weeks to months) — the fibrocartilage is progressively replaced by woven bone through endochondral ossification. Osteoblasts also deposit woven bone directly (intramembranous ossification) where strain is low. The hard callus provides rigid immobilisation.

4. **Remodelling** (months to years) — osteoclasts and osteoblasts remodel the irregular woven bone into organised lamellar bone aligned along stress lines. The medullary cavity is restored. Eventually, the repaired bone is indistinguishable from uninjured bone (in optimal conditions).

**Factors affecting healing:** adequate blood supply, immobilisation, age (children heal faster), nutrition (protein, vitamin C, calcium, vitamin D), absence of infection, smoking (impairs healing), and certain medications (NSAIDs and corticosteroids may delay healing).

## Clinical Relevance

### Common Fracture Patterns

| Fracture | Location | Typical Mechanism | Key Clinical Point |
|----------|----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Colles' fracture | Distal radius, dorsal displacement | FOOSH | "Dinner fork" deformity; check median nerve function |
| Smith's fracture | Distal radius, volar displacement | Fall on back of hand | Reverse Colles' |
| Scaphoid fracture | Scaphoid waist | FOOSH | Anatomical snuffbox tenderness; risk of AVN; may need MRI |
| Clavicle fracture | Junction of middle and lateral thirds | Fall on outstretched hand or shoulder | Most commonly fractured bone; check brachial plexus |
| Femoral neck fracture | Intracapsular neck of femur | Low-energy fall in elderly | Risk of AVN (medial circumflex femoral artery); shortened, externally rotated limb |
| Intertrochanteric fracture | Between greater and lesser trochanters | Fall in elderly | Extracapsular; better blood supply, lower AVN risk |
| Pott's fracture | Bimalleolar or trimalleolar ankle | Forced eversion of foot | Fibula fracture + medial malleolus/deltoid ligament injury |
| March fracture | 2nd or 3rd metatarsal shaft | Repetitive stress (running, marching) | Stress fracture; may not show on initial X-ray |

### Common Pathologies

- **Osteoporosis** — reduced bone density (T-score ≤ -2.5 on DEXA scan) with normal mineralisation; increased fracture risk; most common in postmenopausal women and elderly
- **Osteomalacia / Rickets** — defective mineralisation of osteoid due to vitamin D deficiency or phosphate depletion; osteomalacia in adults, rickets in children (bowed legs, widened epiphyseal plates, rachitic rosary)
- **Paget's disease** — excessive disorganised bone remodelling resulting in enlarged, deformed, weakened bone; elevated serum alkaline phosphatase; risk of osteosarcoma
- **Osteomyelitis** — infection of bone, most commonly by *Staphylococcus aureus*; haematogenous spread (children) or direct inoculation (open fractures, surgery)
- **Osteogenesis imperfecta** — genetic disorder of Type I collagen; brittle bones, blue sclerae, hearing loss; multiple fractures with minimal trauma

## Key Takeaways

- The skeleton provides **support**, **protection**, **movement**, **mineral storage**, **haematopoiesis**, and **energy storage**
- Bone is a **living tissue** with continuous remodelling driven by the balance between osteoblast (formation) and osteoclast (resorption) activity
- **Wolff's Law** — bone adapts to mechanical loading; use it or lose it
- Bone matrix combines organic (collagen — tensile strength) and inorganic (hydroxyapatite — compressive strength) components
- Most bones form via **endochondral ossification** (cartilage model replaced by bone); flat bones form via **intramembranous ossification**
- Blood supply is critical — disruption leads to **avascular necrosis** (femoral head, scaphoid, talus are high-risk)
- Fracture repair follows a stereotyped sequence: haematoma → soft callus → hard callus → remodelling
- Hormonal balance (PTH, calcitriol, oestrogen, cortisol) is essential for maintaining bone homeostasis
